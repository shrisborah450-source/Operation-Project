## 1. Supply Chain and Demand-Supply Management

In service operations, the supply is your team's capacity. The demand is the incoming volume of tickets, cases, and approvals. The job is to make sure supply never falls too far behind demand.

At Capslock, ticket volumes were not uniform. Some days were quiet, some had spikes. My job was to make sure those spikes never became SLA breaches.

### How I Approached It

| Step | What I Did | Result |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Demand Tracking | Monitored daily ticket inflow across all queues and flagged spikes early | Prevented 3 potential SLA breach events |
| Capacity Mapping | Tracked each team member's processing speed to estimate real throughput | Realistic workload distribution instead of uniform assignment |
| Priority Tagging | Tagged tickets as urgent, standard, or low-priority at intake | Reduced urgent resolution time by 30% |
| Backlog Monitoring | Tracked age of open tickets daily so nothing sat unresolved beyond threshold | Consistent SLA adherence across all workflows |
| Cross-team Coordination | Flagged supply gaps to leads before demand exceeded capacity | Prevented downstream failures before they became escalations |

> My Insight: The problem almost never announces itself. Waiting for an SLA breach to react is too late. I got into the habit of checking queue depth every morning, not at the end of the day.

### Budget vs Actuals

- Always negotiate vendor quotes before locking budgets — the first price is rarely the best
- Track actuals weekly, not monthly — by the time monthly reviews happen it is too late to correct overspend
- Build a 10 to 15 percent contingency into every budget for unforeseen costs
- When actuals exceeded budget, I flagged it immediately with a root cause — never buried it in the numbers

## 2. MIS Reporting and KPI Dashboards

I have seen what happens when reporting is treated as a checkbox. Leadership makes decisions on incomplete data, problems get noticed too late, and teams lose credibility.

At Capslock, I owned the weekly MIS reports for senior leadership. At Kaziranga, the dashboards I built were eventually adopted as the standard format across all university departments. 
> A dashboard that is accurate but unreadable is not useful. A dashboard that tells a story — with clear highs, lows, and actions — is what leadership actually needs.

### How I Structure a Weekly MIS Report

| Section | What I Include | Why |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Opening Snapshot | 3 numbers that matter most — one up, one flat, one needs attention | Gets attention immediately, sets the tone |
| SLA Adherence | Percent on-time, percent breached, top 3 breach reasons | Non-negotiable metric always first |
| Quality Metrics | Error rate, rework percent, first-time-right rate | Tracks if speed is coming at cost of accuracy |
| Escalations and Risks | Open escalations, aged items, upcoming risks | Keeps leadership informed before issues become crises |
| Actions and Owners | What needs to happen, who owns it, by when | Every report ends with clear next steps |

> My Insight: The biggest mistake in MIS reports is showing everything equally. Lead with the metric that, if it breaks, everything else breaks. For us, that was SLA adherence.

## 3. Process Improvement

I do not wait to be asked to find problems. I look at the data and ask why things are breaking before leadership has to ask me.

At Capslock, I identified and fixed 3 recurring bottlenecks.

### Bottleneck 1 — Ticket Routing Delays

Problem: Urgent tickets were being processed in the same order as low-priority ones. SLA breach risk was high.

Root Cause: No differentiation at intake. Everything entered the same queue.

Fix: I designed a priority-tagging system at ticket creation. Urgent items went to a dedicated fast-track queue immediately.

Result: Urgent resolution time reduced by 30 percent.

---

### Bottleneck 2 — KYC Rework Due to Unclear Criteria

Problem: Documents were sent back for resubmission because different validators had different standards.

Root Cause: The existing SOP had gaps edge cases were not documented.

Fix: I rewrote the KYC validation SOP with explicit criteria for all 5 document categories and examples of acceptable and rejected submissions.

Result: Zero regulatory breaches and a measurable drop in rework.

---

### Bottleneck 3 — Cross-team Escalations Taking Too Long

Problem: Tickets needing Tech or Compliance input sat in a grey zone with no clear owner.

Root Cause: No defined escalation protocol. People did not know who to contact or by when.

Fix: I built an escalation matrix with named owners per issue type, SLA attached to each escalation level, and a daily blocker check-in.

Result: Cross-team resolution time improved and downstream SLA failures reduced.

---

> My Insight: Every bottleneck I found had the same root cause — a process designed for a simpler environment that was never updated as the team grew. The fix is rarely more people. The fix is a clearer process.
